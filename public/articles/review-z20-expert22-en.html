2. Software and image processing

Both platforms deliver high image quality resulting from their beam-forming architectures, processing algorithms, and noise reduction, signal-to-noise ratio enhancement, and acoustic shadow suppression features. What distinguishes the Samsung HERA Z20 in this generation is the use of Software Beamforming (SWBF) — the ultrasonic beam formation is performed via GPU programming (RTX A4000), instead of conventional dedicated hardware. This architecture enables a feature that until recently was impractical in commercial equipment: the capture of the third harmonic — perhaps the most surprising finding of the Z20 in this review.

What are harmonics in ultrasound

When the transducer emits a fundamental frequency — say, 5 MHz — the tissue does not respond only at that frequency. It generates harmonics: multiples of the original frequency (10 MHz, 15 MHz, etc.). Conventional ultrasound used only the fundamental signal. The second generation brought 2nd harmonic (THI — Tissue Harmonic Imaging), now standard in any modern device: fewer artifacts, more contrast, better edge definition. The 3rd harmonic (three times the fundamental frequency) goes further: even narrower beam, superior lateral resolution, and virtually zero sidelobe artifacts.

The technical challenge is twofold: the 3rd harmonic signal has a frequency 3x higher than the fundamental, but much lower amplitude — and it is precisely this signal that is "wasted" in most equipment. Capturing it requires a robust sampling rate (5 to 10 times higher than the maximum signal frequency) to ensure accurate reconstruction without distortion or aliasing. It is the SWBF with GPU that enables this sampling and processing capability — something that conventional dedicated hardware cannot achieve with the same computational flexibility.

Third harmonic on the Samsung HERA Z20 — exclusive onsite observation

The Samsung HERA Z20 is capable of capturing and utilizing the 3rd harmonic component in specific presets of the CA1-7S (convex) and EV2-12 (endocavitary volumetric) transducers — both using S-Vue (Single Crystal) technology. When active, the system displays a discrete indicator on the HUD. The image improvement is noticeable primarily in the definition of high-frequency structures in the near field — exactly the scenario of fetal neurosonography with endocavitary transducer, where the fetal brain is close and the higher frequency is sustainable.

In onsite practice, fetal neurosonography comparisons showed superior sharpness in the cerebral cortex with the 3rd harmonic active — a real difference, more subtle than conventional harmonic activation, but clinically perceptible for those who know the anatomy. Physics imposes the limit: higher frequencies attenuate more rapidly, making the feature less effective in the far field or in patients with elevated BMI. For fetal neurosonography with endocavitary transducer and fetus in cephalic presentation, the brain is sufficiently close for the 3rd harmonic to be sustained.

Published studies document that the 3rd harmonic provides an improvement of up to 46% in lateral resolution compared to fundamental and 28% compared to the 2nd harmonic [IEEE Xplore, 2012]. Canon Medical (Aplio line) was the first to formalize the technology as a commercial product using a deep learning filter to extract the signal. The Z20 implements its own solution with similar behavior — a finding observed exclusively in this review, during the This Is Us 2026 congress.

3. Volumetric transducers: CMV1-10 versus eM6C G3

The volumetric transducer is the heart of any premium obstetric ultrasound device. Here, the competition is fierce.

Samsung CMV1-10 — single crystal matrix array

The CMV1-10 is the new flagship transducer of the Z20: a convex volume matrix array with S-Vue technology (single crystal), covering 1–10 MHz bandwidth [2,3,4]. This bandwidth is remarkable — it allows from deep penetration in patients with elevated BMI to fine resolution for fetal anatomical details. Samsung does not publish the number of elements, which is a significant gap for technical comparison.

GE eM6C G3 — 8,192 electronic elements

The eM6C G3 is the third generation of GE's electronic matrix probe, with 8,192 elements in an active 2D matrix array, operating at 2–6 MHz [7,8]. The main advantage: being a pure electronic array (no mechanical motor), it enables eSTIC — instantaneous STIC capture in ~3 seconds (versus 7–15 seconds in mechanical probes) [8,9,21]. Real-time 4D volumes with Color Doppler, Bi-Plane and Bi-Plane CRI are exclusive to the eM6C G3.

4. Embedded artificial intelligence: two opposing philosophies

Radar — embedded AI capabilities (scale 0–10)

Samsung HERA Z20 — continuous AI and radical automation

The central feature is Live ViewAssist: deep learning-based AI that operates continuously during the exam, without operator interaction [1,5,6]. It automatically identifies and captures 39 standard planes, annotates 47 anatomical structures and performs 46 essential measurements in real time [5,6]. Samsung claims a reduction of up to 94% in keystrokes in a detailed 2nd trimester exam — a powerful claim, but based on internal data (data on file), not on a peer-reviewed study [5,6].

The FDA validation (510(k) K241971, October 2024) documented Dice scores >0.87 for segmentation and recognition accuracy >94% in diverse datasets [17].

  • BiometryAssist (base): semi-automatic biometry — the operator selects the structure and the system performs the measurement [2,4]
  • ViewAssist (base): automatic biometry — just press the measurement key and the system automatically recognizes the structure
  • HeartAssist (base): automatic classification of fetal cardiac planes, measurements and Z-Scores [2,3,4]
  • Live ViewAssist (standard): real-time ViewAssist — 39 continuous planes, 47 structures, 46 measurements without interaction [5,6]
  • 5D CNS+ (standard): 6 measurements in 3 transverse planes of the fetal brain [3,4]
  • 5D NT (optional): automatic nuchal translucency [4]
  • EzVolume (standard): 3D segmentation of placenta, amniotic fluid, fetal face with color coding [3,4]
  • EzCheck: real-time ISUOG checklist — tracks unacquired planes [2,4]
  • Sonio (cloud, FDA-cleared): AI reports for fetal medicine — partnership since 2024 [5,6]
ℹ️ 5D Heart Color™ — advanced fetal echocardiography
5D Heart Color — AI-automated fetal cardiac analysis

The 5D Heart Color™ is an AI tool that automatically analyzes fetal cardiac structures from a STIC volume. Available on Samsung flagship equipment since 2018, it displays 9 standard fetal echocardiography planes with color Doppler in a single view, using Intelligent Navigation technology to automatically identify and mark structures such as the septum, aorta, and pulmonary artery. In Samsung terminology, "5D" represents the diagnostic dimension — with the 4th dimension being time (volumetric image in motion).

GE Voluson Expert 22 — quantitative ecosystem and advanced echocardiography

GE adopts a modular approach with specialized tools by anatomical area, with emphasis on rigorous quantification and published scientific validation:

  • SonoLyst (Intelligent Ultrasound, UK): automatic recognition of 21 ISUOG planes — up to 65% improvement in efficiency [11]

  • fetalHS: AI-guided cardiac screening — automatic 4-chamber, 3 vessels/trachea and cardiac axis [21]
  • SonoNT / SonoIT: semi-automatic nuchal and intracranial translucency [10,11]
  • SonoVCADheart: 3 clicks for standardized cardiac views from STIC/eSTIC [9,21]
  • Graphicflow: real-time blood cell trajectories — no equivalent on the Z20 [8,9]

5. Display, ergonomics and user experience

The display is a visible and immediate difference. The Samsung Z20 offers a 27-inch OLED monitor with WQHD resolution (2560x1440) — 57% larger than the HERA W10. The OLED provides absolute blacks and infinite contrast, particularly valuable for fetal echocardiography and 3D/4D rendering [1,4,36]. The GE Expert 22 uses a 23.8-inch HDU LCD monitor in Full HD (1920x1080) — curiously a step back from the E10, which had OLED [7,12,13].

My hands-on experience with both devices
⚠️ Bias disclosure

I have been a GE user for over 20 years. I have worked with GE devices for a long time and have deep familiarity with the Voluson platform. This inevitably creates an experience bias — especially in knobology and workflow. This review was built with a commitment to technical honesty, but the reader should keep this context in mind when weighing my impressions.

B-mode image

In B-mode, both devices are very similar to each other — and do not present a significant evolution compared to previous mid-range versions, such as the Voluson S10. Pure B-mode image quality is not where these devices differentiate themselves markedly.

GE's larger screen makes a real difference in clinical practice. But Samsung's OLED delivers something that LCD cannot replicate: superior contrast, with brightness highlights that make the visual experience more comfortable — provided the operator takes care to properly calibrate the brightness. For someone who spends 10 hours a day in front of an ultrasound monitor, this is not a minor detail.

Knobology and ergonomics

This is where my bias becomes more evident — and I need to be honest about it. On the GE, the experience is surreal: my hands go directly to the controls like muscle memory, without any cognitive effort. The workflow accelerates significantly precisely because of this. It's not that Samsung is bad — it's that GE, for me, is invisible.

Regarding the motorized height and lateral control, both devices are excellent. The only negative point I identified on the GE was the auxiliary touchscreen without tilt control — Samsung, on the other hand, has tilt on the 15.6" touchscreen, which represents a point in favor of ergonomics. This was confirmed by the Samsung representative after the congress.

About the transducers: the GE eM6C G3 has the same weight as the previous mechanical probe — no regression. Samsung's CMV1-10 seemed slightly lighter to me. In both, the grip is good and did not cause discomfort during handling.

Artificial intelligence in practice — what really matters

In terms of speed and processing, including AI functions, the two devices are surprisingly close in subjective experience. I did not perceive a significant performance difference between them.

What impressed me the most about both devices — and what I consider the most relevant AI advancement applied to ultrasound so far — was not the automatic measurements, nor the rendering, nor the fine adjustments. It was the checklist with real-time image validation: the system alerts when a mandatory plane has not yet been acquired and, upon acquiring it, evaluates whether the image is technically correct — whether the 4-chamber view, for example, contains all the elements it should contain. This exists in both devices. And it is the type of tool that changes the diagnostic quality standard silently and consistently.

The automatic auto-adjustment works reasonably well. But the experienced ultrasonographer will always feel compelled to make micro knobology adjustments — and is right to do so. AI will improve greatly over time, but it does not replace the specialist. On the contrary, it amplifies what they already do well.

Samsung has a differentiator that I was unable to explore in depth due to limited time with the device: the ability to create and customize your own checklists and workflows. For those who have extended access to the equipment, this could be a real differentiator. Noted for the record.

3D/4D rendering

No significant visual difference between the two — and I say this candidly. The evolution in this area has been, in large part, marketing-driven. Samsung delivers a texture improvement in 3D images processed on the device itself via AI — something conceptually similar to what models like Stable Diffusion do with static images (I even have a dedicated section on this at drmassuca.com.br). It is a real improvement, but not a leap.

Image sharing with the patient via mobile phone exists on both devices — a feature available on Samsung since 2018. It is a nice feature, but it is not what will define the future of AI in ultrasound.

Samsung's 5D Heart Color™

What I saw at the congress was the demonstration of 5D Heart Color™ — a tool available on Samsung devices since 2018, which generates 9 standard fetal echocardiography planes with color Doppler from a STIC volume, with automatic structure identification via AI. The technology is mature and clinically established. What I await with real interest is EzHQ — expected for the 2nd half of the year — which will bring automated quantification of fetal heart global strain (GLS), placing Samsung in a directly competitive position with GE's fetalHQ2 in this segment.
